Bauhaus

1919–1933

The Bauhaus, founded in 1919 by Walter Gropius in Weimar, unified art, craft, and industrial production within a single pedagogical framework. Its foundation course, the Vorkurs, introduced students to materials, color theory, and geometric abstraction before specialization. Guided by functionalism and the principle “form follows function,” Bauhaus rejected ornament in favor of modular composition and structural clarity. Faculty such as László Moholy-Nagy advanced experimentation in typography and photography. When the school closed in 1933, its faculty dispersed internationally, spreading modernist design principles worldwide.

Vorkurs functionalism geometric abstraction modular composition form follows function

Examples: MoMA — Bauhaus Collection · MoMA — Walter Gropius

Not to be confused with Art Deco, because Art Deco embraced ornament and luxury materials while Bauhaus reduced design to structural and functional essentials.

Swiss Style

International Typographic Style · 1950s–1970s

Swiss Style, also known as the International Typographic Style, emerged in the 1950s and emphasized objective communication through grid systems and sans-serif typography. Designers such as Josef Müller-Brockmann structured compositions using asymmetrical layout and precise typographic hierarchy to achieve visual order. Photography replaced illustration to reinforce clarity and neutrality. The baseline grid ensured alignment and consistency across layouts. Swiss Style sought universal readability by minimizing authorial expression in favor of rational structure.

grid system sans-serif typography asymmetrical layout typographic hierarchy objective typography

Examples: MoMA — Josef Müller-Brockmann, Musica Viva (1958) · MoMA — Armin Hofmann, Giselle (1959)

Not to be confused with Bauhaus, because Bauhaus centered on interdisciplinary education and craft integration while Swiss Style refined typographic systems for mass communication.

Accountability: I verified this by checking MoMA’s collection records for Josef Müller-Brockmann and Armin Hofmann.

New Wave Typography

1970s–1980s

New Wave Typography developed in the 1970s as a reaction against the strict grid discipline of Swiss Style. Designers expanded typographic experimentation through layering, variable spacing, and expressive typography while still referencing modernist structure. Rather than abandoning the grid entirely, New Wave manipulated alignment systems to introduce movement and spatial tension. This approach preserved modernist foundations while challenging the neutrality of objective design.

grid disruption typographic layering expressive typography baseline grid spatial tension

Examples: Cooper Hewitt — April Greiman · MoMA — April Greiman

Not to be confused with Grunge Typography, because New Wave experimented within modernist structure while Grunge later rejected legibility and polish.
